Deep Blue

The Artificial intelligence that defeated a Chess World Champion

Deep Blue was the name given to an artificial intelligence developed to win matches against human world-class chess champions. Originally developed under the name 'ChipTest' in 1985 at Carnegie Mellon University by Feng-hsiung Hsu along with a team he assembled for the project. Its name later changed to 'Deep Thought' before gaining its final name after battling against chess-champion Kasparov for the first time in 1989. After over a decade of work by Feng-hsiung Hsu and his team, Deep Blue was able to defeat world-class chess champion Garry Kasparov, becoming an icon of the industry.

Deep Blue's development began in 1985 when doctoral student Feng-hsiung Hsu of Carnegie Mellon University began work on ChipTest, the first iteration of this chess-playing supercomputer. After just two years of development, ChipTest went undefeated at the World Chess Computer Champsionship.

In 1988, Hsu's team's development on ChipTest brought it into a new evolution, Deep Thought. A year later, after Hsu graduated and joined IBM along with teammate Murrary Campbell, a match was arranged by IBM against Garry Kasparov, a renowned chess champion. Deep Thought lost 0-2.

Soon after, Deep Thought was once again renamed, and became 'Deep Blue' after IBM's nickname 'Big Blue'.

In 1996, Deep Blue's first formal match against Kasparov, an eight-day, six-game match, ended in a 2-4 loss, with 1 win, 2 draws, and 3 losses.

One year later, a final rematch against Kasparov brought Deep Blue's victory: 3½ to Kasparov's 2½, with 2 wins, 3 draws and 1 loss

After defeating Kasparov and cementing Deep Blue's legendary status as a feat of computer science and engineering, Deep Blue is dismantled by IBM, which permanently denied Kasparov's demand for a rematch
